The Invention of the Internet

Brief history of the Internet invention goes like...

  • Inclusion guided its development. Intended to be as open as possible where anyone could contribute or use.
  • Starting 1950s, idea behind Internet was envisioned by a psychologist called JCR Licklider, a professor at MIT. His idea was that computers could be linked together which would allow us to communicate better and would be beneficial in sharing of information.
  • Computers would have remained standalone monoliths, if not for USSR’s launch of Sputnik in October 1957 that spurred the scientists into the idea of teamwork.
  • To catchup and surpass the Soviet Union in space technology, President Eisenhower created a division at the Defense Department call Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA), in charge of the space program which included Computer Science.
  • Establishment of NASA gave an impetus to the space race but left research into Computer Science behind.
  • It moved slowly but kept making steady progress.
  • Leonard Kleinrock, a graduate student at MIT, began applying queuing theory to data transmission.
  • Demand Access and Distributed Control, an idea for effective resource sharing and shared control respectively are key principles.
  • Paul Baran was hired to see if a resilient network could be created which would withstand nuclear attack.
  • Came up with “hot potato” routing which ensures a path remains viable even if part of the network is destroyed.
  • Key is to break messages into small, equal sized chunks called packets which included a to and from address.
  • Packet switching research and theory matured in 1962 to show that it could be built and used for computer communication. Actual implementation, in fact, didn’t happen until 1969.
  • In 1966, Bob Taylor, of ARPA, needed a separate terminal to log into each separate timeshare computers spread across the country. They didn’t talk to each other.
  • He envisioned a network where one terminal could connect to all the computers that were networked together.
  • Taylor recruited Larry Roberts for building the network. Roberts had already built an experimental connection between two computers at an MIT library.
  • The contract to build the network based on Roberts’ instruction was awarded to BBN. The initial plan was to network 4 computers, at UCSB, SRI, UCLA and Utah together on this network. Work began in January 69 with first delivery planned 9 months later.
  • First IMP, interface message processor, arrived at UCLA in September 1969.
  • All 4 sites were interconnected by end of 1969.
  • By April 1971, 18 mainframes were on the ARPANet network.
  • Raymond Tomlinson introduced email on the network in March 1972.
  • October 1972 ARPA demonstrated the computer network in Washington, DC.
  • In growing the Internet, the focus was on ideas and dialogue rather than edicts and directives.
  • By 1972 ARPANet had fewer than 25 sites online yet more were coming. What sparked an interest was the first International Conference on Computer Communication sponsored by ARPA.
  • NSF required to be on their network to receive grant money.
  • LANs and WANs emerged to connect offices and branches across buildings, campuses and institutions.
  • In 1973 Vin Cerf and Bob Kahn teamed up to develop a set of rules to connect disparate networks together, called TCP/IP protocol.
  • Tim Berners-Lee created World Wide Web in 1980. Allowed Internet to be free and user friendly.
  • In 1983, after 10 years of refinement, TCP/IP was adopted as the universal standard. This allowed the rapid evolution of the Internet, a common network of interconnected networks.
  • In June 1992 Congress passed legislation taking the Internet out of the hands of the Government and into public hands.
  • 50 web pages on the Internet in 1992.
  • Mosaic web brower developed 1993 by Marc Andreesen to allow users to “browse” the Internet. Allowed the “web” to grow 341,000% shortly after.
  • It is still under construction and will always be.
  • The history doesn’t capture the potential of the Internet…it’s grown and reach is only limited by the human imagination and ingenuity.
